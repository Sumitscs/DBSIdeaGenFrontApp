Can the alternative trading Facilities survive?


Global stock exchange industry of late is going through rough weather after the emergence of faster Multilateral Trading Facilities (MTF). It is interesting to see that multilateral trading facility Chi-X has recently announced good profits and this makes one think about how they will fare in the next year, as all the other new MTFs come in. So far Chi-X has been successful but surely times will become tougher with greater competition.

All MTFs broadly offer similar services and currently USP of all of them is speed and low cost of their trading engines. Assuming that speed is the single most benchmark that all the MTFs are aiming for, how important is the speed? Speed should not be the primary reason why any trader would prefer one trading venue over another. Low latency can be an advantage for a sell side customer as it helps to grab quick arbitrage opportunities. However, low latency can not be a privilege of a few MTFs in the longer term. Due to the improvements in technology, speed will be available to all players and it will become a commodity. Speed will boost algorithmic trading which offers enormous value to the customer. But it can never replace the human brain in assessing the overall direction of the market and the gut feeling that separates good traders from the ordinary.

All the emerging MTFs offer very similar Clearing capabilities with Equiduct having the greater flexibility for the customer. The Stock Exchanges will be able to compete with MTFs on speed and low cost but also have the important factors of liquidity and better risk management capabilities behind them and they are not going to rollover and let their business drip away. MTFs would soon realize that liquidity and risk management capabilities can not be built overnight and it will take lot of time, hard work and credibility building performance to achieve that.

So, what could be the motive behind sudden emergence of so many MTFs? They are trying to cash in on speed and cost factor which could give them an edge over exchanges in the short term. Lower revenues will put pressure on exchanges who would try to buy these MTFs. It is likely that an MTF expects to be bought by another bigger MTF or more likely a Stock Exchange before liquidity and costs come into it. So is it just a game of setting up a business and system only to sell for a profit as soon as possible? Only time can answer this question!!

By Sivaji Nagandla

“It’s the time for Firms to re-organize themselves for the end of the economic crisis.”

As the sub-prime meltdown completes its first anniversary, the question that comes to the mind: What should be the strategy for firms for next year? Where should they invest their efforts and their precious IT resources? To answer this question, we need to look at the crux of the problem. There are three key market factors that went wrong which led to the market crisis: a) Due to the prevail of low interest rates for long term, lenders offered products which they should not have to people who should not have bought them; b) competition forced the credit rating agencies issue ratings on complex products which they didn't fully understand; and c) the poor timing of a global economic Crisis.

So, what is the right way to handle the situation? First and foremost, there is no need to panic. While IT budgets will be trimmed, downturns and dislocations happen; the industry will survive. Governments will still need to borrow, corporations still need to raise capital, and individuals still need homes to live. This means that investment banks are still required to lead manage issue of equity and debt, and fund managers are still required to invest their funds. Then, how should the 2009 technology budget be allocated?

First, firms must improve their trading and market data infrastructures, as well as move these facilities to other asset classes and geographies. Investment in cutting edge trading infrastructure is crucial: either one invest to stay competitive or risk being forced out of the market totally. Investment in modern electronic trading is currently in full swing in Europe and Asia in futures, stock options, FX and even in fixed income securities.

Though firms continue to invest in modern trading infrastructure, such expenditure accounts only for a small percentage of their total technology investment. In fact, the bulk of firms' technology investments lie in their processing capabilities, and investment bank write-offs will transition the industry from revenue driven strategies to cost driven strategies.

Investment banks usually spend substantial amount in acquiring state of the art technology but often are poor at re-engineering these platforms into their infrastructures. While going is good, it is more profitable to invest in the next billion-dollar business than rebuild platforms. Platform rationalization needs to occur periodically, and this tends to occur only during a slump in the markets. Now is the time to examine which platforms are strategic, which are tactical, which are still useful and which can be done away with.

To add to the above, firms must attempt at reduction of costs through further automation. At the moment, the most significant operational challenges would be in the OTC derivatives business. From front office to middle to back office, much of the current OTC derivatives processing is complex, non-standardized and human intensive. Automation and re-engineering of these processes and technology infrastructure would yield significant profits. Automation and re-engineering involve not only leveraging newer order execution venues and central clearing facilities, but also better integrating derivatives technologies with existing systems. While focusing investments in derivatives technologies, firms will hike their investment in risk management also as this will aid firms in better control of their existing positions.

Till date, as the crisis shows no signs of letting up, we still need to focus on the larger picture: Financial Markets Industry is instrumental to the financial health of the planet.  Though it may look like we are sinking into endless depths, fundamentals will help markets find a bottom and it can only get better from then. So need of the hour is to keep focus on the basics, rationalize the infrastructure and prepare for a new dawn.

By Sivaji Nagandla
